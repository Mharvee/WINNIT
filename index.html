Why you can trust this draw

No spinning ball machine, no studio lights — just a process anyone in Lagos can check for themselves.
Verifiable process
🎲 Is the draw really random?
Yes. A random seed is sealed and time-stamped before the draw starts, so it can't be picked to favour anyone.
🙅 Can Winit staff choose who wins?
No — nobody can see or influence the result before the seal opens after the draw.
🎫 How do I know my ticket really entered?
Your ticket is marked "Confirmed" the moment payment clears, visible under My Tickets before, during and after the draw.
Licensed & regulated
🏛️ Is this legal in Nigeria?
Winit operates under a registered company (CAC) and a lottery license from [state regulator] — both numbers are published on the draw archive screen for anyone to check.
Lagos-based support
💬 Can I reach a real person?
Yes — WhatsApp and phone support, staffed from our Lagos office, not a chatbot that never replies.
Presentation controls
The core reframe
The lock isn't a restriction — it's the pitch

Instead of quietly disabling purchases at T-20s, the design names it out loud, twice, before it happens (lobby countdown label, boost screen status bar) so it lands as "they built this so nobody can cheat" instead of "why did my payment fail?" A rule a user doesn't understand reads as suspicious; the same rule explained in advance reads as proof.

Biggest lever for Lagos specifically
The anti-scam card on the Verify screen

The single most common reason a Nigerian ignores a "you won" message is advance-fee fraud — fake winner alerts that ask for a "clearance fee" or a PIN. Stating explicitly, in the app itself, everything Winit will never ask for turns your own product into the thing that teaches users to distrust the scam messages, not yours.

Handled carefully
Odds transparency, not pressure

"Boost your odds" is real and honest — more tickets genuinely means better odds, shown with real maths. What's deliberately absent: fake scarcity ("only 3 spots left"), countdown-induced panic copy, or hiding the day's spend. The spend line is a small, non-judgmental transparency habit borrowed from Nigerian fintech apps users already trust — it costs little and reads as a platform that isn't trying to trap them.
